# Fuji Speedway

Fuji Speedway sits at the foot of Mt. Fuji in Oyama, Shizuoka Prefecture, and
first opened for racing in 1966. The original circuit famously included a steep
banked section; after Toyota took ownership the track was substantially
redesigned by Hermann Tilke and reopened in 2005 as the modern Grand Prix
circuit. That layout runs clockwise over 2.835 mi (4.563 km) and sixteen turns,
climbing and falling around the foothills of the mountain.

## The main straight and Turn 1

Fuji's identity is its main straight — at roughly 1.475 km (0.917 mi), one of
the longest in motorsport. Cars reach their highest speed of the lap before
braking hard for the tight right-hander at Turn 1, now carrying Toyota Gazoo
Racing (TGR) branding. It is the circuit's marquee overtaking zone and its most
common accident spot, where a clean exit from the final corner pays off all the
way down the front straight.

## 100R and the fast opening

The early part of the lap flows downhill through the off-camber Coca-Cola Corner
and into 100R, a fast, sweeping corner of roughly 100-metre radius that is one
of the most respected on the circuit and near-flat in a high-downforce car. The
high-speed 300R sweep follows before the lap slows for the infield.

## The technical final sector

Where the long straight is all bravery, the closing sequence is all precision: a
slow, technical infield through the sponsor-named GR Supra and Dunlop corners
winds toward the uphill Panasonic Corner, the medium-speed final right-hander
that releases the cars back onto the main straight to begin it all again.
