# Daytona International Speedway

Daytona International Speedway opened in 1959, conceived by NASCAR founder Bill
France Sr. and engineered by Charles Moneypenny as a 2.5-mile tri-oval with
31-degree banked turns. The sports-car road course threads a tight infield
section into most of that oval: 3.56 mi (5.729 km) across twelve turns, run
counterclockwise and essentially unchanged since the infield was revised in
1985. Endurance racing arrived with the Daytona Continental in 1962, a race
that grew into the Rolex 24 At Daytona, run around the clock since 1966.

## The banking

What separates Daytona from every other stop on the sports-car calendar is the
banking. From the exit of Turn 6 the cars run flat-out around the NASCAR
oval's Turns 1 and 2, down the backstretch, and through Turns 3 and 4 — long
stretches held at 31 degrees, interrupted only by the Le Mans Chicane — before
the 18-degree tri-oval completes the lap past the start/finish line.

## The Le Mans Chicane

The backstretch chicane, known for decades as the Bus Stop, is the road
course's signature complex: a left-right entry and right-left exit reached at
near-maximum speed. It was widened in 2003 and lost its third leg in 2010, and
in January 2022 it was renamed the Le Mans Chicane in a reciprocal gesture with
Circuit de la Sarthe, whose first chicane on the Mulsanne Straight became the
Daytona Chicane.

## The infield

The infield opens with an ever-tightening left-hander at Turn 1 — the lap's
prime overtaking spot — and is anchored by two 180-degree right-hand hairpins:
the Rodríguez International Horseshoe (Turn 3), which carries the name of
Mexican sports-car great Pedro Rodríguez, and the West Horseshoe (Turn 5),
linked by the fast left-hand Dogleg. Motorcycles use a modified infield with a
tighter hairpin, but the cars' twelve-turn course is the layout recorded here.
