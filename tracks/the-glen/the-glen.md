# Watkins Glen International

Watkins Glen International opened as a permanent circuit in 1956 above the
village of Watkins Glen in New York's Finger Lakes region, the successor to the
street races that had run on public roads there since 1948. Laid out with help
from the Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory and engineer William Milliken, "the
Glen" became the home of the United States Grand Prix from 1961 to 1980 and is
now owned by NASCAR.

This is the **Long Course** — the endurance "Boot" configuration. It measures
3.450 mi (5.552 km) over eleven turns, with roughly 41 m (135 ft) of elevation
change, and is the layout used by IMSA's 6 Hours of the Glen. It differs from
the Short Course (used by the NASCAR Cup race) by adding the wooded four-turn
Boot extension built for 1971; the Inner Loop "bus stop" chicane that defines
the modern circuit was added in spring 1992.

## The Esses and the Outer Loop

From the heavily banked downhill right of Turn 1 — "The 90" — the lap climbs
through the Esses, the fast, uphill right-left-right sequence that is the corner
the Glen is best known for: the middle left crests blind over the brow of the
hill, carried near flat-out and rewarding a driver who keeps the car settled as
the camber rolls beneath it. The long back straight follows, broken late by the
1992 Inner Loop "bus stop" chicane, before the long, steeply banked right of the
Outer Loop — NASCAR's Carousel — sweeps the cars downhill into the Boot.

## The Boot

The Outer Loop drops the cars down the Chute, a steep downhill left, into the
Boot itself — the wooded four-turn extension built for 1971. The near-180 of the
Toe of the Boot climbs hard out of the trees to the slower right of the Heel,
where the extension rejoins the original circuit. A quick left out of the Boot
and a fast left at Turn 10 lead to the final right of Turn 11 — its exit wall
close enough to concentrate the mind — releasing the cars onto the main straight.
