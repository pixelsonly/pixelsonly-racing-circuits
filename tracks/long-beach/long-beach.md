# Long Beach Street Circuit

The Grand Prix of Long Beach is the oldest and best-known street race in
American motorsport. Travel agent and racing enthusiast Chris Pook conceived it
in the mid-1970s to revitalize the downtown Long Beach waterfront, and the first
event ran in 1975 for Formula 5000 cars. Formula One followed from 1976 to 1983
before the race switched to CART/IndyCar in 1984, where it has remained the
centerpiece of the calendar ever since. The Grand Prix Association of Long Beach
still organizes the event, now under Penske Entertainment ownership.

The circuit is a temporary street course laid out on the city streets around the
Long Beach Convention Center. The current Grand Prix configuration, settled
around 2000, measures 1.968 mi (3.167 km) over eleven turns, run clockwise. Like
every version since 1975 it is anchored by the long Shoreline Drive front
straight along the waterfront.

## Shoreline Drive and Turn 1

The fastest moment of the lap is the run down Shoreline Drive into Turn 1. Cars
reach roughly 170 mph along the waterfront front straight before braking hard
for the tight 90-degree left-hander (the headline overtaking spot, where the
race is routinely decided) which spills the field into the fountain complex.

## The Fountain and the Hairpin

After Turn 1 the cars thread the slow fountain complex (Turns 2–3), curling
around the landmark Long Beach dolphin fountain between flowerbeds and concrete
walls. The circuit's signature corner is the hairpin at Turn 11: a tight,
first-gear right-hander taken at around 30 mph and arguably one of the most
famous corners in IndyCar. It has anchored every layout since 1975. Reached
after the long, sweeping left of Turn 10, the hairpin dominates lap time and
setup; getting it right launches the car back onto Shoreline Drive toward the
start/finish line.
