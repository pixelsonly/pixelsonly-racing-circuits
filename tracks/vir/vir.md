# Virginia International Raceway

Virginia International Raceway opened on August 3, 1957 on rolling farmland at
Alton, Virginia, near Danville. After decades dark, the land spent years as
cow pasture, the circuit was painstakingly restored and reopened in March 2000,
and today ranks among the finest road courses in the United States. The Full
Course runs clockwise for 3.270 mi (5.262 km) over seventeen turns, with about
130 ft (40 m) of elevation change and straights of 3,000 and 4,000 feet linking
a lap of blind crests and sweeping, connected bends.

## The Climbing Esses

VIR's identity is the Climbing Esses (Turns 7–9), the signature sequence and,
by common account, the most exciting feature of the course. The cars climb the
hillside through a rapid run of alternating bends, each cresting near its apex
so the car goes light over the rises: a section that rewards rhythm, minimal
steering input over the hilltops and absolute trust in the camber.

## Oak Tree, the Roller Coaster and Hog Pen

Turn 11 is still called Oak Tree for the lone oak that stood on its outside until
it was removed in 2012; the corner feeds the long back straight, making it one of
the most important on the lap. The Roller Coaster (Turn 14) crests a hill and is
often likened to a scaled-down mirror of Laguna Seca's Corkscrew, while NASCAR
Bend (Turn 3), a tricky decreasing-radius left, carries its name from the
trouble it gave Richard Petty, David Pearson and Wendell Scott in a 1966 Trans-Am
race. The lap closes through the Snake and the fast downhill Hog Pen (Turn 17),
the final right-hander that fires the cars back onto the front straight.
