# Sebring International Raceway

Sebring International Raceway sits on the former Hendricks Field, a WWII heavy-bomber
training base near Sebring, Florida. Russian-American aeronautical engineer Alec
Ulmann, inspired by the 24 Hours of Le Mans, laid a race course over the old runways
and taxiways, and the first event, the Sam Collier Six Hours Memorial, ran on New
Year's Eve, 1950. The Grand Prix Road Course measures 3.74 mi (6.019 km) over
seventeen turns, run clockwise, and has hosted the 12 Hours of Sebring, one of the
world's great endurance races, ever since.

## The runway surface

Sebring's defining feature is its surface: long sections of aged, seam-ridden concrete
runway spliced with patches of asphalt. The result is famously rough and bumpy, brutal
on cars and drivers alike across twelve hours, and it is the reason a Sebring finish
is treated as a test of durability as much as speed.

## Turn 17: Sunset Bend

The circuit's identity is its final corner. Universally called "Turn 17," Sunset Bend
is a long, extremely bumpy high-speed right-hander wide enough to run cars three
abreast, named for the setting sun drivers face here late in the race. Carrying speed
through its battered concrete and onto the front straight is the single hardest thing
to get right at Sebring, and a clean Turn 17 can define a lap or a stint.

## A lap of legends

The lap reads like a roll-call of sports-car history: Gurney Bend (Turn 6), the slow
Hairpin (Turn 7), the flat-out Fangio chicane (Turns 8–9), Cunningham Corner (Turn 10),
Collier Curve (Turn 11), the bumpy Tower Turn (Turn 13), the intimidating off-camber
Bishop Bend (Turn 14), Gendebien Bend (Turn 15), and the lap-critical Le Mans Corner
(Turn 16) that opens onto the back straight. Most are named for drivers, owners, and
founders who shaped the 12 Hours.
